### Handling Live Cargo Emergencies
One of the most extreme scenarios involves live cargo, such as the crocodiles shown in official footage. When a crate breaks, the cargo specialist must act immediately.

1. **Clear the Area**: Move passengers away from the cargo hold entrance.
2. **Isolate the Threat**: Use cabin dividers or physical objects to trap the animal.
3. **Secure the Crate**: Repair the containment unit before attempting to return the cargo.
4. **Report to Pilot**: Ensure the pilot maintains a level flight to prevent the animal from sliding into other crew members.

When an alarm sounds, the crew must follow a standardized protocol to ensure the aircraft's safety. Use these steps to manage any crisis effectively.

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Detection and Communication">
    Immediately call out the type and location of the emergency (e.g., "Fire in the rear galley!"). The Pilot should acknowledge and check for upcoming turbulence.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="Immediate Containment">
    The nearest crew member drops their current task to address the threat. If it is a fire, use the extinguisher. If it is an escaped animal, block the aisle.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Stabilization Maneuvers">
    The Pilot adjusts the flight trim to minimize the impact of physics on the crew's movement. Avoid banking turns while the cabin crew is unbuckled and active.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="Secondary Damage Control">
    Once the primary threat is neutralized, check for secondary issues like injured passengers, loose luggage, or structural hull damage that requires external repair.
  </Step>
  <Step num="5" title="Cabin Reset">
    Return to routine service only after all hazards are secured and the Pilot confirms the weather is clear. Re-seat any passengers who were moved during the crisis.
  </Step>
</Steps>
