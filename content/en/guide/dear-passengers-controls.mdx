Operating the aircraft requires precise inputs to balance the plane's pitch, roll, and yaw while managing engine output. The table below outlines the core control inputs for both the cockpit and the cabin environments.

| Role | Action Type | Primary Control Input | Operational Impact |
|------|-------------|-----------------------|--------------------|
| **Pilot** | Flight Steering | Flight Stick / WASD | Adjusts pitch and roll |
| **Pilot** | Throttle Control | Throttle Lever / Mouse | Manages airspeed |
| **Pilot** | Systems Toggle | Cockpit Switches | Toggles lights and landing gear |
| **Cabin** | Movement | WASD / Left Stick | Navigates the physics-heavy cabin |
| **Cabin** | Grab / Interact | Primary Action Key | Picks up food, luggage, or tools |
| **Cabin** | Use Item | Secondary Action Key | Extinguishes fires or repairs hulls |
| **Cabin** | Restrain | Hold Interact Key | Subdues unruly passengers |

<Section icon={Target} title="Step-by-Step Flight Setup and Control Procedure">

Completing a flight requires a structured sequence of actions. Your team must transition from pre-flight planning to active flight management without losing control of the cabin. 

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Choose Passengers and Cargo">
    Before takeoff, use the terminal controls to select your load. Balancing risk and reward is key. Higher-paying flights feature more demanding passengers or highly volatile cargo.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="Assign Crew Stations">
    Designate one player to the cockpit controls while the remaining players position themselves in the cabin and cargo hold. Ensure everyone knows their primary interaction zone.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Execute Takeoff Controls">
    The pilot must increase throttle, maintain a steady climb angle, and retract the landing gear once airborne. Cabin crew should stand by to secure loose objects immediately.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="Manage Mid-Flight Service">
    Cabin players must interact with food carts and drink dispensers to serve passengers. Keep the aisles clear of loose luggage to maintain fast movement pathways.
  </Step>
  <Step num="5" title="Respond to Emergencies">
    When alarms sound, drop routine tasks. Use emergency tools like fire extinguishers or weapons to neutralize immediate threats to the aircraft's hull.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="tip" title="Co-op Pro-Tip">
Always have a designated backup pilot. If the primary pilot is knocked out by extreme turbulence or an external hazard, a cabin crew member must quickly access the cockpit controls to prevent a nose dive.
</Callout>

</Section>
